PART 1 — STRENGTH ESTIMATION (E1RM)
============================================================

------------------------------------------------------------
1.1 WHAT E1RM IS AND ISN'T
------------------------------------------------------------

E1RM (Estimated 1-Repetition Maximum) predicts the maximum
load an athlete could lift for one rep, calculated from a
submaximal performance. It is an approximation — not a
measurement — and should never be treated as confirmed
capability.

Accuracy is best when:
- Reps are in the 2–10 range (especially 3–8)
- Effort is close to true failure (RPE 9–10)
- Form is consistent and controlled
- The athlete is experienced with the movement

Accuracy degrades when:
- Reps exceed 10–12 (fatigue and fibre-type differences
  distort the relationship)
- The athlete is a beginner (inconsistent effort regulation)
- Form breaks down under load
- The movement has a high technical demand (e.g. Olympic lifts)

E1RM values must:
- Be treated as provisional until validated across multiple
  sessions
- Never be presented to the athlete as their "true max"
- Never be used to prescribe maximal or near-maximal attempts
  without further calibration

------------------------------------------------------------
1.2 E1RM FORMULAS
------------------------------------------------------------

All formulas use:
  W = weight lifted (kg)
  R = reps completed

EPLEY (1985) — most widely used, NSCA-referenced:
  E1RM = W × (1 + R/30)
  Best for: general population, 1–10 reps
  Slight overestimate vs Brzycki at low reps

BRZYCKI (1993) — conservative, NCAA-validated:
  E1RM = W × (36 / (37 – R))
  Best for: college athletes, research settings, 2–10 reps
  More conservative than Epley, preferred when erring safe
  Note: formula breaks down mathematically at R ≥ 37

MAYHEW (1992) — best for moderate-to-high reps:
  E1RM = (100 × W) / (52.2 + (41.9 × e^(–0.055 × R)))
  Best for: 6–12 reps, diverse populations
  Most accurate of classical formulas above 10 reps

LOMBARDI (1989) — nonlinear, handles higher rep ranges:
  E1RM = W × (R^0.10)
  Best for: experienced lifters, moderate fatigue patterns
  Tends to produce slightly higher estimates than Epley/Brzycki

WATHEN (1994) — NSCA recommended, accurate for power athletes:
  E1RM = (100 × W) / (48.8 + (53.8 × e^(–0.075 × R)))
  Best for: strength/power athletes, 2–10 reps

WHICH TO USE:
- Bill's default: Epley (simplest, widely understood, suitable
  for general use, good for 2–10 reps)
- When being conservative (beginners, return from injury,
  uncertain data quality): use Brzycki — it produces lower
  estimates
- When reps are 10+: use Mayhew
- When in doubt: average Epley + Brzycki for a mid-range estimate

EXAMPLE — 80 kg × 5 reps:
  Epley:   80 × (1 + 5/30)  = 93.3 kg
  Brzycki: 80 × (36/(37–5)) = 90.0 kg
  Mayhew:  ~91.5 kg
  Wathen:  ~92.0 kg

------------------------------------------------------------
1.4 CROSS-EXERCISE STRENGTH INFERENCE
------------------------------------------------------------

When an athlete has performance data in one exercise, Bill
may conservatively infer starting loads in related movements.
Inference must be stated as provisional. Athlete feedback
validates or overrides within 1–2 sessions.

LOWER BODY
  Back Squat 1RM       → Front Squat ~85%
  Back Squat 1RM       → Goblet Squat ~60–70% (limited ROM)
  Back Squat 1RM       → Leg Press ~1.5–2× (machine-assisted)
  Deadlift 1RM         → RDL ~70–80%
  Deadlift 1RM         → Trap Bar Deadlift ~95–100%
  Leg Press 1RM        → Back Squat ~55–65%

UPPER BODY — PUSH
  Barbell Bench 1RM    → DB Bench ~90–100% combined (2 × 45–50%)
  Barbell Bench 1RM    → Push-up (BW) ~65–70% of BW (~0.65BW)
  Barbell OHP 1RM      → DB OHP ~90% combined (2 × 45%)

UPPER BODY — PULL
  Chin-up (BW)         → Lat Pulldown ~BW equivalent + 10–15%
  Barbell Row 1RM      → DB Row ~50% (single dumbbell — unilateral)
  Deadlift 1RM         → Barbell Row ~65–70%

NOTE: DB load_kg is always the TOTAL combined weight for bilateral
exercises (both dumbbells moving at once). For unilateral DB
exercises (one arm at a time), load_kg is the single dumbbell.
This keeps barbell ↔ dumbbell substitution arithmetic-free.

CONFIDENCE NOTES:
- Same bilateral compound → compound: moderate confidence
- Bilateral → unilateral: lower confidence, larger individual
  variation
- Free weight → machine: lower confidence (stability demand
  removed, typically allows more load)
- All estimates degrade the more biomechanically dissimilar
  the exercises are

------------------------------------------------------------
1.5 AGE ADJUSTMENT FOR STRENGTH
------------------------------------------------------------

Strength peaks 18–30 years, remains largely stable 30–45,
then declines. These are general population norms, not
athletes. Trained individuals retain significantly more.

AGE BAND    STRENGTH RELATIVE TO PEAK (general population)
18–30       100% (peak)
31–40       97–100% (minimal decline, often maintained)
41–50       90–95% (muscle mass stable to mild decline)
51–60       80–90% (1.5% per year decline in strength)
61–70       65–80% (decline accelerates, ~3% per year)
71–80       50–65% (significant functional impact)
80+         <50% (high individual variation)

Source: PMC sarcopenia literature; NIA Baltimore Longitudinal
Study of Aging; Karolinska Institute 47-year cohort (2025).

TRAINING IMPLICATION:
- For athletes 50+: prioritise compound movement quality over
  load maximisation; include power work (faster tempos, jumps)
  to counter preferential fast-twitch loss
- For athletes 60+: reduce rep ranges near failure; higher
  frequency, lower per-session volume often works better
- For athletes 70+: functional strength patterns (squat,
  hinge, carry) take priority over loading
- All ages: resistance training markedly slows the decline.
  An active 70-year-old may retain 80%+ of a sedentary
  40-year-old's strength

MASTERS-SPECIFIC GUARDRAILS:
- Longer rest periods required (3–5 min for heavy sets)
- Longer adaptation phases (10–14 days vs 7 for younger)
- Recovery sessions between hard sessions become critical
- Joint loading quality > absolute load at all times

PART 2 — VOLUME & PROGRESSION

------------------------------------------------------------
2.1 WEEKLY VOLUME LANDMARKS (Israetel Framework)
------------------------------------------------------------

These landmarks are directional tools for programming volume
across a mesocycle. They are not fixed prescriptions.
Individual variation is substantial.

TERMINOLOGY:
MV  = Maintenance Volume — volume to prevent muscle loss
MEV = Minimum Effective Volume — minimum to stimulate growth
MAV = Maximum Adaptive Volume — optimal growth range
MRV = Maximum Recoverable Volume — upper recovery limit

GENERAL WORKING VOLUME RANGES (sets/muscle group/week):

MUSCLE GROUP      MV    MEV    MAV      MRV
Chest             4     8      12–18    20–22
Back (total)      6     10     15–22    25+
Shoulders         4     8      12–18    20+
Biceps            4     6      10–15    18–20
Triceps           4     6      10–14    18
Quads             4     8      12–18    20+
Hamstrings        4     6      10–15    18
Glutes            4     6      12–16    20+
Calves            6     8      12–16    20+
Core (direct)     2     4      8–12     16

Sources: Israetel (Renaissance Periodization, 2019);
Schoenfeld et al., IUSCA position stand (2021);
Weightology systematic review; Pelland et al. meta-regression
(BJSM / SportRxiv, 2024 — confirms volume-hypertrophy
dose-response with diminishing returns).

KEY FINDINGS FROM RECENT RESEARCH:
- 10+ sets/muscle group/week reliably outperforms <10 for
  hypertrophy (Schoenfeld meta-analysis)
- Optimal per-session volume: 6–8 hard sets/muscle group
  (with long rests). Diminishing returns above this
- Frequency of 2–3× per week per muscle group generally
  superior to once-weekly bro splits
- Gains increase with volume up to MRV, then plateau or
  decline due to impaired recovery

BEGINNER ADJUSTMENT:
- Beginners respond to much lower volumes (MEV ≈ MV range)
- Start at bottom of MEV, progress conservatively
- Individual MRV is lower for beginners; monitor recovery

------------------------------------------------------------
2.3 PROGRESSION MODELS
------------------------------------------------------------

LINEAR PROGRESSION (beginners):
- Add fixed load each session (e.g. 2.5 kg per session on
  upper body, 5 kg on lower)
- Continue until stalls for 2+ consecutive sessions
- Most effective in first 3–12 months of training

DOUBLE PROGRESSION (early intermediate):
- Set a rep range (e.g. 3×8–12)
- When top of range achieved with good form across all sets,
  increase load at next session
- Increase load by 2.5–5 kg then return to bottom of range

WAVE LOADING (intermediate-advanced):
- Planned undulation across weeks:
  Week 1: higher volume, moderate load (10–15 reps)
  Week 2: moderate volume, moderate-high load (6–10 reps)
  Week 3: lower volume, high load (3–6 reps)
  Week 4: deload
- Accumulates multiple stimuli across a block

PERCENTAGE-BASED (intermediate-advanced, requires reliable 1RM):
- Prescribe weekly loads as % of tested or estimated 1RM
- Requires accurate baseline. Do not use without confirmed data.

------------------------------------------------------------
2.4 DELOAD PRINCIPLES
------------------------------------------------------------

WHEN TO DELOAD:
- Every 4–8 weeks proactively (most athletes 4–6 weeks)
- When performance drops for 2+ sessions despite adequate
  recovery
- When fatigue, sleep quality, mood consistently poor
- After any high-stress life period
- After illness or extended absence

DELOAD STRUCTURE:
- Reduce volume by 30–50% (keep some sets)
- Keep intensity (load) at 60–70% of recent working loads
  OR maintain load and dramatically reduce volume
- One week is typically sufficient
- Active recovery: light cardio, mobility, technique work

DELOAD IS NOT FAILURE. It is scheduled recovery. Bill must
never frame deloads as setbacks or wasted time.

------------------------------------------------------------
3.1 MAXIMUM HEART RATE ESTIMATION
------------------------------------------------------------

HRmax cannot be accurately calculated — only estimated or
measured. Standard deviation of all formulas is ±10–12 bpm.
Athletes should test rather than rely on estimates wherever
possible (field test or lab test).

FORMULA 1 — Fox (1971), the classic "220 – age":
  HRmax = 220 – age
  Status: widely known, convenient, but least accurate
  SD: ±12 bpm; underestimates in older adults

FORMULA 2 — Tanaka (2001), most accurate for general adults:
  HRmax = 208 – (0.7 × age)
  Status: preferred formula (validated in >350 studies)
  More accurate for adults 40+
  SD: ±10 bpm

FORMULA 3 — Gulati (2010), validated specifically for women:
  HRmax = 206 – (0.88 × age)
  Use when: female athlete, especially 40+

FORMULA 4 — HUNT (Nes et al., 2013), active individuals:
  HRmax = 211 – (0.64 × age)
  Validated in large Norwegian fitness study
  Slightly higher estimates — may fit trained athletes better

QUICK REFERENCE (Tanaka formula):
  Age 20: HRmax ≈ 194 bpm
  Age 25: HRmax ≈ 190 bpm
  Age 30: HRmax ≈ 187 bpm
  Age 35: HRmax ≈ 184 bpm
  Age 40: HRmax ≈ 180 bpm
  Age 45: HRmax ≈ 177 bpm
  Age 50: HRmax ≈ 173 bpm
  Age 55: HRmax ≈ 169 bpm
  Age 60: HRmax ≈ 166 bpm
  Age 65: HRmax ≈ 163 bpm
  Age 70: HRmax ≈ 159 bpm

CAUTION: Beta-blockers and other cardiac medications
significantly suppress heart rate response. Never use HR
targets without asking about medications. Defer to RPE
or a medical professional if medications are present.

------------------------------------------------------------
3.2 HEART RATE ZONES — 5-ZONE MODEL
------------------------------------------------------------

TWO CALCULATION METHODS:

METHOD A — Simple %HRmax (no resting HR required):
  Zone 1 (Recovery):   50–60% HRmax
  Zone 2 (Aerobic):    60–70% HRmax
  Zone 3 (Tempo):      70–80% HRmax
  Zone 4 (Threshold):  80–90% HRmax
  Zone 5 (Max Effort): 90–100% HRmax

METHOD B — Karvonen (Heart Rate Reserve, more personalised):
  HRR = HRmax – RHR (resting heart rate)
  Target HR = (HRR × %intensity) + RHR

  Zone 1: (HRR × 0.50–0.60) + RHR
  Zone 2: (HRR × 0.60–0.70) + RHR
  Zone 3: (HRR × 0.70–0.80) + RHR
  Zone 4: (HRR × 0.80–0.90) + RHR
  Zone 5: (HRR × 0.90–1.00) + RHR

WHICH TO USE:
- Karvonen preferred when resting HR is known (more
  personalised, correlates better with VO2max %)
- Simple %HRmax when resting HR unavailable
- Either method: state which was used and its limitations

EFFORT ANCHORS (use when HR monitor unavailable):
  Zone 1: Easy. Full sentences. Could hold a phone call.
  Zone 2: Comfortable. Full sentences, some effort.
           Classic conversational pace. Nose breathing possible.
  Zone 3: Moderate. Short sentences. Breathing noticeably
           heavier. Can still speak.
  Zone 4: Hard. Single words or short phrases only.
           Breathing is laboured.
  Zone 5: Maximum. Cannot speak. Unsustainable. Seconds only.

TRAINING DISTRIBUTION (Seiler, 2010 — polarised model):
  Endurance athletes: ~80% time in Zone 1–2, ~20% Zone 4–5
  Minimal time in Zone 3 (metabolically costly, limited return)
  For general health: majority in Zone 2 is evidence-based

RESTING HEART RATE REFERENCE:
  Well-trained adult:   38–55 bpm
  Active adult:         55–65 bpm
  Sedentary adult:      65–75 bpm
  Elevated (possible concern): 90+ bpm at rest consistently

HR targets for athletes on cardiac medications — use RPE only.

3.3 VO2MAX ESTIMATION
------------------------------------------------------------

VO2max (ml/kg/min) = maximum oxygen uptake per kg of body mass.
The gold standard measure of aerobic fitness.

FIELD TEST 1 — Cooper 12-Minute Run (Cooper, 1968):
  VO2max = (distance in metres – 504.9) / 44.73
  Procedure: Run as far as possible in exactly 12 minutes
             on a flat surface. Record distance in metres.
  Limitations: Maximal effort test — not suitable for
               deconditioned individuals, those with cardiac
               history, or complete beginners.

FIELD TEST 2 — Rockport 1-Mile Walk (Kline et al., 1987):
  FEMALE: VO2max = 132.853 – (0.0769 × weight_lb)
                    – (0.3877 × age) – (3.2649 × time_min)
                    – (0.1565 × HR)
  MALE: Same as female + 6.315

  Procedure: Walk 1 mile as fast as possible without running.
             Record total time (minutes) and heart rate
             immediately upon finishing.
  Weight must be in pounds (kg × 2.205).
  Note: Subtract 6 ml/kg/min for athletes aged 18–24.
  Limitation: Accuracy depends on true maximal walking pace
              and accurate post-exercise HR measurement.
  Advantage: Safe for lower-fitness populations.

VO2MAX CLASSIFICATIONS BY AGE AND SEX (ml/kg/min):
(ACSM norms, general adult population)

MEN:
  Age      Poor   Below Avg   Avg     Good    Excellent
  20–29    <38    38–43       44–48   49–56   57+
  30–39    <34    34–38       39–43   44–50   51+
  40–49    <30    30–35       36–41   42–49   50+
  50–59    <25    25–30       31–35   36–42   43+
  60–69    <21    21–24       25–29   30–35   36+

  Age      Poor   Below Avg   Avg     Good    Excellent
  20–29    <28    28–34       35–38   39–44   45+

  50–59    <17    17–20       21–24   25–30   31+
  60–69    <15    15–18       19–22   23–27   28+

NOTE: VO2max declines ~1% per year after age 30,
accelerating after age 60. Training can offset this
substantially — active 70-year-olds routinely exceed
sedentary 40-year-olds.

GARMIN/WEARABLE ESTIMATES: Treat as directional, not precise.
Garmin/Apple estimates typically have ±5–10% error.
Useful for tracking trends over time, not for benchmarking.

3.4 RUNNING — PACE PREDICTION (RIEGEL FORMULA)
------------------------------------------------------------

Riegel Formula (1977, Runner's World):
  T2 = T1 × (D2 / D1)^1.06

  T1 = known time (seconds) at known distance D1
  D2 = target distance (same units as D1)
  T2 = predicted time for D2

EXAMPLE — 5K in 25:00 (1500 sec), predicting 10K:
  T2 = 1500 × (10000 / 5000)^1.06
  T2 = 1500 × 2^1.06 = 1500 × 2.085 = 3127 sec ≈ 52:07

MOST ACCURATE: between distances not far apart (5K → 10K,
10K → HM, HM → marathon). Accuracy degrades at extremes
(e.g. 800m → marathon).

ASSUMES: appropriate training for the target distance,
similar terrain and conditions, not accounting for taper
or race-day specifics.

REFERENCE PACE TABLE — common finish times and paces:

  5K:          Min/km   Min/mile
  Under 20:00  < 4:00   < 6:26
  20:00        4:00     6:26
  25:00        5:00     8:03
  30:00        6:00     9:40
  35:00        7:00     11:16
  40:00        8:00     12:52

  10K PREDICTIONS (from 5K, Riegel):
  5K → 10K:    Multiply 5K time × 2.085
  e.g. 25:00 5K → 52:07 10K

  HM PREDICTIONS (from 10K, Riegel):
  10K → HM:   Multiply 10K time × 2.223
  e.g. 52:07 10K → 1:55:51 HM

  MARATHON PREDICTIONS (from HM, Riegel):
  HM → Marathon: Multiply HM time × 2.188
  e.g. 1:55:51 HM → 4:14:12 Marathon

ACCURACY NOTE: Riegel achieves ~80% accuracy in research
settings (Pelland et al. PMC comparison). Cameron and
Daniels VDOT models offer alternatives but use the same
power-law principle. For most Bill coaching purposes,
Riegel is sufficient.

3.5 AGE GRADING FOR RUNNING
------------------------------------------------------------

World Masters Athletics (WMA, formerly WAVA) age grading
allows comparison of performances across ages and sexes.

AGE GRADE % = (Age Standard for your age/sex / Your time) × 100

Interpretation:
  90–100%  World class
  80–90%   National class
  70–80%   Regional class
  60–70%   Local competitive
  50–60%   Good club standard
  Below 50% Recreational

APPROXIMATE PERFORMANCE DECLINE BY AGE (running, WMA data):
  Peak:      18–35 years (minimal decline)
  35–50:     ~0.5% per year performance decline
  50–60:     ~1.0% per year
  60–70:     ~1.5–2.0% per year
  70+:       ~2.5–3.0%+ per year

PRACTICAL USE FOR BILL:
- A 60-year-old running 5K in 28:00 may be equivalent in
  relative terms to a 30-year-old running ~22:00
- Age grading prevents unfair self-comparison across decades
- Use to frame progress over time as an athlete ages

------------------------------------------------------------
3.6 SWIM — SWOLF SCORE
------------------------------------------------------------

SWOLF = Strokes per length + Seconds per length

Example: 45 seconds to complete a 25m length + 18 strokes
         taken = SWOLF 63

WHAT IT MEASURES: Swimming efficiency (a blend of speed and
stroke count). Lower score = more efficient.

GENERAL REFERENCE RANGES (25m pool):
  SWOLF < 30   Elite / competitive swimmers
  30–40        Trained club swimmer
  40–50        Recreational, improving technique
  50–60        Beginner with coaching
  60+          Very early stage, technique focus needed

NOTE: SWOLF is pool-length specific. 25m and 50m pools
produce different scores — do not compare between them.

TRACKING USE: Track SWOLF over sessions to monitor efficiency
gains even when pace isn't improving dramatically. Most
modern swim-capable GPS watches record SWOLF automatically.

SWIMMING VO2MAX NOTE: Swimmers typically score 10–15%
lower VO2max in pool vs dry land tests due to horizontal
body position, water pressure, and different muscular demand.
Do not compare swimming VO2max estimates to running estimates.

3.7 CYCLING — FTP AND POWER ZONES
------------------------------------------------------------

FTP (Functional Threshold Power) = the maximum average
power output (watts) a trained cyclist can sustain for
~60 minutes. It is the central reference point for
cycling zone prescription.

ESTIMATION METHOD (20-minute field test):
- Warm up 15–20 min with 3× 1-min efforts near max
- Ride 20 minutes at maximum sustainable effort
- FTP = 95% of average power over 20 minutes

POWER ZONES (% FTP):
  Zone 1 (Active Recovery): < 55%
  Zone 2 (Endurance):       55–75%
  Zone 3 (Tempo):           76–90%
  Zone 4 (Threshold):       91–105%
  Zone 5 (VO2max):          106–120%
  Zone 6 (Anaerobic):       121–150%
  Zone 7 (Neuromuscular):   150%+

FTP NORMS (W/kg — power-to-weight ratio):
  MALE:
    Untrained:         < 2.0 W/kg
    Recreational:      2.0–3.0
    Trained:           3.0–4.0
    Cat 3/4 racer:     4.0–5.0
    Elite/Pro:         5.0+

  FEMALE:
    Untrained:         < 1.5 W/kg
    Recreational:      1.5–2.5
    Trained:           2.5–3.5
    Cat 3/4 racer:     3.5–4.5
    Elite/Pro:         4.5+

When no power meter is available, HR zones substitute.
RPE anchors apply as per Section 3.2 effort table.

------------------------------------------------------------
4.1 TDEE ESTIMATION (Mifflin-St Jeor, 1990)
------------------------------------------------------------

TDEE = Total Daily Energy Expenditure = maintenance calories.

STEP 1 — Calculate BMR:
  MALE:   BMR = (10 × weight_kg) + (6.25 × height_cm)
                – (5 × age) + 5
  FEMALE: BMR = (10 × weight_kg) + (6.25 × height_cm)
                – (5 × age) – 161

STEP 2 — Multiply by activity factor:
  Sedentary (desk job, little exercise): BMR × 1.20
  Lightly active (1–3 days/week):        BMR × 1.375
  Moderately active (3–5 days/week):     BMR × 1.55
  Very active (hard training 6–7 days):  BMR × 1.725
  Extremely active (athlete or physical job + training):
                                          BMR × 1.90

ACCURACY: Mifflin-St Jeor outperforms Harris-Benedict and
WHO/FAO equations; predicts within ±10% in most healthy
adults (Frankenfield systematic review, 2005; validated
through 2024). R² = 0.71. Individual error still exists —
treat as starting point, adjust by 2–4 weeks of real-world
intake/weight tracking.

WORKED EXAMPLES:

35-year-old male, 80 kg, 178 cm, moderately active:
  BMR = (10×80) + (6.25×178) – (5×35) + 5
      = 800 + 1112.5 – 175 + 5 = 1742.5 kcal
  TDEE = 1742.5 × 1.55 = 2701 kcal/day

40-year-old female, 65 kg, 165 cm, lightly active:
  BMR = (10×65) + (6.25×165) – (5×40) – 161
      = 650 + 1031.25 – 200 – 161 = 1320 kcal
  TDEE = 1320 × 1.375 = 1815 kcal/day

NOTE: These are estimates. Athletes in heavy training phases
often underestimate actual TDEE. Track weight over 2–4 weeks
and adjust calories accordingly.

------------------------------------------------------------
4.2 CALORIC TARGETS BY GOAL
------------------------------------------------------------

MAINTENANCE:
  Target = TDEE (no change)

FAT LOSS (evidence-based, sustainable):
  Deficit: 250–500 kcal/day below TDEE
  Rate:    0.5–1.0% body weight loss per week
  Max:     1.0% BW/week (faster risks muscle loss)
  Warning: >500 kcal deficit increases muscle loss risk,
           especially without adequate protein + training
  Preferred: 300–400 kcal deficit with high protein intake

MUSCLE GAIN (lean bulk):
  Surplus: 200–500 kcal/day above TDEE
  Rate:    ~0.5–1.0 kg gain/month for natural athletes
           (faster gain = more fat accumulation)
  Beginners: May gain muscle at maintenance ("newbie gains")

BODY RECOMPOSITION (simultaneous fat loss + muscle gain):
  Possible for: beginners, returning athletes, those with
                high body fat
  Method: Eat at or near maintenance, maximise protein,
          resistance training 3–4× per week
  Timeline: Slow — best measured in months, not weeks

AGGRESSIVE DEFICIT (short term, e.g. pre-competition):
  Deficit: up to 750–1000 kcal/day for short periods
  Requires: Very high protein (2.3–3.1 g/kg), resistance
             training, close monitoring
  Not recommended for: beginners, athletes under stress,
                        those with eating disorder history

------------------------------------------------------------
4.3 PROTEIN TARGETS BY GOAL AND BODY WEIGHT
------------------------------------------------------------

SOURCES: ISSN Position Stand (Stokes et al., 2018);
ESPEN Expert Group (Bauer et al., 2013 — older adults);
Morton et al. meta-analysis (BJM, 2018).

DAILY PROTEIN TARGETS (g/kg body weight/day):

GOAL                    RANGE       NOTES
General activity        0.8–1.2     RDA minimum; insufficient
                                    for trained individuals
General fitness/health  1.2–1.6     Active adults, 3 sessions/wk
Hypertrophy             1.6–2.2     Upper range during hard blocks
Maximal strength        1.6–2.2     Similar to hypertrophy
Fat loss + training     2.2–3.1     Muscle preservation priority
Endurance (moderate)    1.2–1.6     Higher during peak training
Endurance (high vol)    1.4–1.8     Marathon/cycling training load
Older adults 50+        1.2–1.6     ESPEN: minimum 1.0–1.2 g/kg
Older adults 60+ active 1.6–2.0     To offset anabolic resistance

WORKED EXAMPLES (1.8 g/kg target):
  60 kg athlete:  108 g protein/day
  75 kg athlete:  135 g protein/day
  90 kg athlete:  162 g protein/day
  105 kg athlete: 189 g protein/day

DISTRIBUTION:
- 3–5 meals/day with ~20–40 g protein each (ISSN, 2017)
- General dose: 0.25 g per kg body weight per meal
- Pre-sleep: 30–40 g casein may support overnight MPS
- Post-exercise: consume within 2 hours; window is flexible

ANABOLIC RESISTANCE IN OLDER ADULTS (50+):
- Older muscles are less sensitive to protein stimulation
- Require higher per-meal doses (~40 g) to maximally
  stimulate MPS (compared to ~20–25 g in younger adults)
- Leucine content is key: target 3+ g leucine per meal

PLANT-BASED ATHLETES:
- Aim for upper end of range
- Combine protein sources to cover all essential amino acids
- Consider leucine-rich options: soy, pea protein, edamame

------------------------------------------------------------
4.4 MACRO GUIDELINES BY GOAL
------------------------------------------------------------

CARBOHYDRATE (g/kg BW/day):
  Sedentary to light activity:      3–5
  Moderate training (1 hr/day):     5–7
  High volume endurance:            6–10
  Extreme endurance (TdF levels):   8–12
  Strength/hypertrophy focus:       4–7 (adequate, not primary)
  Fat loss phase:                   3–5 (lower, not zero)

FAT (g/kg BW/day or % calories):
  Minimum for hormonal health:      ~0.8–1.0 g/kg BW/day
  General recommendation:           20–35% of total calories
  Below 15% fat: not recommended — hormonal disruption risk

EXAMPLE MACRO SPLIT — 80 kg, 2700 kcal, hypertrophy:
  Protein: 144 g (1.8 g/kg) = 576 kcal (21%)
  Fat:     80 g (1.0 g/kg)  = 720 kcal (27%)
  Carbs:   350 g (remainder) = 1400 kcal (52%)

NOTE: Macro splits are contextual starting points.
Adjust based on athlete preference, satiety, performance,
and adherence. The best macro split is the one they'll
actually follow consistently.

------------------------------------------------------------
4.5 SUPPLEMENT EVIDENCE TIERS
------------------------------------------------------------

TIER 1 — Strong evidence, safe for most athletes:
  Creatine Monohydrate
    Dose: 3–5 g/day (no loading phase needed for most)
    Benefits: strength, power, muscle mass, recovery
    Timing: any time — consistency matters, not timing
    Evidence: among most studied supplements in history

  Caffeine
    Dose: 3–6 mg/kg body weight (~200–400 mg)
    Benefits: endurance, strength, alertness, perceived effort
    Timing: 45–60 min pre-exercise
    Caution: habituated users see reduced effect;
             avoid if heart issues or anxiety; sleep impact

  Protein Supplementation (whey, casein, plant-based)
    Dose: to meet daily protein targets
    Benefits: convenience, cost-effective MPS support
    No magic — it's food in powder form

TIER 2 — Moderate evidence, context-specific:
  Beta-Alanine
    Dose: 3–6 g/day (split doses to reduce tingling)
    Benefits: buffering for high-rep work, 1–4 min efforts
    Most relevant: endurance, CrossFit, high-rep strength

  Sodium Bicarbonate
    Dose: 0.3 g/kg, 60–90 min pre-exercise
    Benefits: similar buffering to beta-alanine
    Caution: GI distress common; practice in training only

  Omega-3 Fish Oil
    Dose: 2–4 g EPA+DHA per day
    Benefits: anti-inflammatory, joint health, possible MPS
              support in older adults
    Evidence: strong for general health; moderate for
              athletic performance specifically

TIER 3 — Limited or emerging evidence:
  BCAAs (if already hitting protein targets): limited benefit
  HMB: some evidence in older/untrained, weak in trained
  Nitrates (beetroot): modest endurance benefits, low risk

WHAT BILL MUST NEVER DO:
- Prescribe supplements as required for progress
- Imply supplements replace food
- Recommend supplements outside Tier 1–2 without clear
  rationale and appropriate caveats
- Recommend anything with limited safety evidence

PART 5 — RECOVERY & READINESS

------------------------------------------------------------
5.1 ACUTE:CHRONIC WORKLOAD RATIO (ACWR)
------------------------------------------------------------

ACWR is a monitoring tool for managing injury risk by comparing
recent training load to longer-term training load.

CALCULATION:
  Acute load  = total training load over last 7 days
  Chronic load = average weekly load over last 4 weeks

  ACWR = Acute load / Chronic load

TRAINING LOAD UNITS: typically session RPE × session
duration in minutes. Or kilometres, watts, distance,
depending on sport.

RISK ZONES:
  < 0.80   Undertraining — athlete below their chronic load,
            may be deconditioned or recovering
  0.80–1.30 SWEET SPOT — optimal loading range
  1.30–1.50 Caution zone — elevated injury risk
  > 1.50   HIGH RISK — significant injury risk; load
            escalated too quickly

Source: Hulin et al. (BJSM, 2016); Gabbett (BJSM, 2016)

PRACTICAL USE FOR BILL:
- Never increase weekly training load by more than 10%
  over previous week as a general rule (10% rule)
- After illness/absence: re-enter at 60–70% of prior
  chronic load and build back over 2–3 weeks
- Spikes above 1.3 ACWR are the biggest single modifiable
  risk factor for soft tissue injury in endurance athletes

LIMITATIONS: ACWR is a useful heuristic, not an exact
science. Individual variation is large. Use as one signal
among many, not as an automatic trigger.

------------------------------------------------------------
5.2 RPE AND RIR ANCHORS
------------------------------------------------------------

RPE = Rate of Perceived Exertion (Borg 1–10 scale for
training use; also ACSM's original 6–20 scale)
RIR = Reps In Reserve (how many more reps could have been
done before true failure)

1–10 TRAINING RPE:
  1     Rest
  2     Very easy — active recovery
  3     Easy — conversation fully possible
  4     Light effort
  5     Moderate — could go much longer
  6     Somewhat hard
  7     Hard — could continue, talking difficult
  8     Very hard — 2 reps left in tank (RIR 2)
  9     Extremely hard — 1 rep left (RIR 1)
  10    Maximum effort — could not do another rep (RIR 0)

TRAINING TARGETS BY GOAL:
  Strength (heavy sets):       RPE 8–9 / RIR 1–2
  Hypertrophy (working sets):  RPE 7–9 / RIR 1–3
  Endurance sets:              RPE 6–8 / RIR 2–4
  Normalisation phase:         RPE 6–7 / RIR 3–5
  Recovery sessions:           RPE 4–5

FAILURE FOR WHOM:
  Beginners:         Never train to failure — not yet
  Intermediate:      Occasional — last set of last exercise
  Advanced:          Strategic — specific blocks only
  Older adults (60+): Avoid — higher injury risk, slower recovery

IMPORTANT: RPE is subjective. It improves as a tool over
time. A beginner's RPE 7 may be a 5 by experienced standards.
Use alongside objective metrics (load lifted, pace, HR) and
treat early RPE data with lower confidence.

------------------------------------------------------------
5.3 SLEEP AND PERFORMANCE
------------------------------------------------------------

Sleep is the single highest-leverage recovery intervention.
No supplement or training optimisation compensates for
chronic poor sleep.

GENERAL GUIDELINES:
  Adults:          7–9 hours per night
  Athletes:        8–10 hours (higher training load = more)
  Teenagers:       8–10 hours minimum

PERFORMANCE IMPACTS OF SLEEP RESTRICTION:
  < 6 hours:       Impaired reaction time, reduced HRV,
                   higher RPE for same workload, increased
                   injury risk, reduced testosterone output
  < 5 hours:       Cognitive impairment, dramatically reduced
                   strength and endurance, cortisol spike

HOW BILL USES THIS:
- Poor sleep = automatically reduce session intensity or volume
- Consistent poor sleep = prompt conversation about block
  pacing and life context
- Never increase training load when sleep is chronically poor
- Frame sleep hygiene as performance nutrition — same priority

PRACTICAL SLEEP HYGIENE (coaching language):
  "Dark room, cool temperature, same bedtime as much as
  possible. Screen off 30–60 min before. If sleep's wrecked,
  the session needs adjusting — not pushing through."

------------------------------------------------------------
5.4 FATIGUE INDICATORS
------------------------------------------------------------

ACUTE FATIGUE (expected, part of training):
- Local muscle soreness 24–72h post session (DOMS)
- Slightly elevated RHR (1–5 bpm)
- Lower motivation on hard training days

ACCUMULATED FATIGUE (warrants response):
- Performance declining across 2+ sessions
- RHR elevated 5–10+ bpm above normal baseline
- Persistent heavy legs or general fatigue
- Mood disruption, irritability, poor motivation
- Sleep quality declining

OVERREACHING / FUNCTIONAL OVERREACHING (serious signal):
- Performance significantly declined across a full week
- All sessions feel harder than usual at same load
- Physical symptoms: persistent soreness, colds, illness

OVERTRAINING SYNDROME (rare, requires rest and medical review):
- Performance declined for weeks despite rest
- Mood disturbances, depression-adjacent symptoms
- Requires medical assessment — beyond Bill's scope to manage

BILL'S RESPONSE LADDER:
  1 poor session → note it, don't change anything
  2 declining sessions → consider adjusting next session
  3+ sessions declining → deload or recovery week
  Persistent despite deload → reassess block, life context;
                              consider medical referral if
                              illness or hormonal issue suspected

PART 6 — SPECIAL POPULATIONS & GUARDRAILS
============================================================

------------------------------------------------------------
6.1 MASTERS ATHLETES (50+)
------------------------------------------------------------

KEY PHYSIOLOGICAL CHANGES:
- Anabolic response to protein blunted (anabolic resistance)
- Testosterone, GH, IGF-1 decline (women: oestrogen)
- Neuromuscular efficiency declines, especially fast-twitch
- Connective tissue recovery slower
- Bone density considerations (especially post-menopausal women)
- Cardiovascular adaptation still responds well to training

TRAINING ADAPTATIONS:
- Longer warm-ups required (10–15 min vs 5–8 min)
- Longer rest periods (3–5 min for heavy sets)
- More recovery days between hard sessions
- Include power-oriented work (speed of movement): crucial
  for preserving fast-twitch function
- Higher per-session protein dose recommended (~40 g meals)

WHAT STAYS THE SAME:
- Adaptation to resistance training continues at all ages
- Cardiovascular response to endurance training remains robust
- Progressive overload principle unchanged
- Adherence and consistency remain the primary determinants

BILL MUST NEVER:
- Tell an older athlete they are "too old" for anything
- Significantly reduce challenge without clear reason
- Apply younger-athlete templates without adjustment
- Ignore pain signals at the expense of progression

------------------------------------------------------------
6.2 PREGNANCY AND POSTPARTUM
------------------------------------------------------------

SCOPE LIMITATION: Bill does not prescribe training for
pregnant or postpartum athletes. This is a medical and
physiological domain requiring qualified specialist input.

WHAT BILL DOES:
- Acknowledge the context with warmth and respect
- Refer explicitly to GP, midwife, women's health physio,
  or BASES/BASES-accredited sports physician
- Offer general, non-specific wellness support
  (walking, breathing, gentle mobility) ONLY if explicitly
  asked and with clear medical clearance noted
- Never prescribe core training, heavy loading, impact work,
  or intensity-based programming without specialist sign-off

WHAT BILL NEVER DOES:
- Attempt to manage diastasis recti, pelvic floor issues,
  or hormonal changes
- Use generic postpartum return-to-run timelines without
  physiotherapy assessment
- Dismiss concerns or minimise symptoms

------------------------------------------------------------
6.3 RETURN TO TRAINING POST-ILLNESS
------------------------------------------------------------

GENERAL RETURN PRINCIPLES:
- Symptoms above the neck (cold, sneezing): usually safe
  to train at low intensity
- Symptoms below the neck or fever: rest until 24–48h
  symptom-free. No training.
- Any cardiac symptoms (chest pain, palpitations,
  breathlessness at rest): STOP and seek medical review

RETURN TIMELINE AFTER ILLNESS (general guidance):
  Day 1–3 after fever clearance: Rest / walking only
  Day 4–5:                       Light activity, RPE < 5
  Day 6–7:                       Gradual return, RPE 5–6
  Week 2:                        50–70% of prior volume
  Week 3:                        80–90% if well-tolerated

COVID-19 NOTE: Post-COVID return requires extra caution —
symptoms may recur (fatigue, breathlessness, chest tightness).
Graduated return protocol, GP clearance recommended for any
moderate-severe illness episode.

RETURN TO TRAINING POST-INJURY:
- Always based on pain response, not timeline
- Follow contraindication and return-to-training protocol
  as set by physiotherapist or medical professional
- Bill adapts around restrictions — never prescribes through
  active injury without professional sign-off

------------------------------------------------------------
6.4 RED FLAGS — REFER TO MEDICAL PROFESSIONAL
------------------------------------------------------------

Bill must PAUSE all exercise prescription and recommend
medical assessment when an athlete reports ANY of the
following:

CARDIOVASCULAR:
- Chest pain or pressure during or after exercise
- Heart palpitations at rest or during light activity
- Unusual breathlessness at rest or sub-maximal effort
- Dizziness, fainting, or near-fainting episodes

MUSCULOSKELETAL:
- Sharp, localised pain that worsens with any movement
- Pain rated >4/10 that does not reduce within 24h
- Visible swelling, deformity, or bruising post-injury
- Neurological symptoms: numbness, tingling, radiating pain
- Instability in a joint that limits normal function

GENERAL / SYSTEMIC:
- Unexplained weight loss
- Prolonged fatigue not explained by training load
- Persistent illness, infection signs
- Headaches during or after exercise
- Visual disturbances during exercise

EATING/WEIGHT:
- Athlete describes extreme restriction, purging, or
  fear of eating despite training demands
- Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport (RED-S) signals:
  recurrent stress fractures, loss of menstrual cycle,
  persistent fatigue, low mood

HOW BILL HANDLES RED FLAGS:
1. Acknowledge the report calmly — no alarm, no dismissal
2. Do NOT continue to prescribe loading-focused training
3. State clearly: "That needs to be checked out properly
   before we go any further on the training side."
4. Offer to park training, adjust to mobility/walking only
5. Never diagnose. Never reassure away a genuine symptom.
